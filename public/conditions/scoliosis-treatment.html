Understanding Scoliosis

Scoliosis is a three-dimensional spinal condition characterized by a sideways curvature of the spine that measures 10 degrees or more on X-ray. Beyond the visible curve, scoliosis involves rotation of the vertebrae and can affect the entire spine's biomechanics, rib cage position, and overall posture.

The condition affects approximately 2-3% of the population, with the majority of cases being idiopathic (meaning the cause is unknown). While scoliosis is often discovered during adolescence, it can affect people of all ages and may progress differently in each individual.

Beyond the Curve: Scoliosis isn't just about the degree of curvature. It affects how your spine moves, how your muscles work, how your nervous system communicates, and ultimately how you feel in your body. Our approach addresses these functional aspects to help you live more comfortably.

Types of Scoliosis

Idiopathic Scoliosis

The most common form, accounting for about 80% of cases. The cause is unknown, though genetic factors play a role. It's typically categorized by age of onset:

  • Infantile: Diagnosed before age 3
  • Juvenile: Diagnosed between ages 3-10
  • Adolescent: Diagnosed between ages 10-18 (most common)
  • Adult: May be a continuation of adolescent scoliosis or develop later in life

Congenital Scoliosis

Present at birth due to vertebral malformations that occur during fetal development. The spine may have vertebrae that are incompletely formed, fused together, or absent. This type requires specialized orthopedic monitoring from an early age.

Neuromuscular Scoliosis

Develops as a result of neurological or muscular conditions such as cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy, or spinal cord injuries. The curve develops because the muscles cannot properly support the spine.

Degenerative Scoliosis

Also called adult-onset or de novo scoliosis, this develops in adulthood due to disc degeneration, osteoporosis, or spinal arthritis. It's increasingly common as people age and can cause significant discomfort.

How Scoliosis Affects the Nervous System

The Spine-Brain Connection

Your spine does more than provide structural support; it houses and protects your spinal cord, the primary communication pathway between your brain and body. When the spine curves abnormally, several things can happen:

  • Altered proprioception: The brain receives distorted information about body position and posture
  • Asymmetric muscle signaling: Muscles on each side of the spine receive different neurological input
  • Compensatory patterns: The body develops adaptive patterns that can cause additional stress
  • Autonomic effects: The curve may influence organs and systems controlled by nerves exiting at affected levels
  • Chronic stress response: Pain and postural strain can keep the nervous system in a heightened state

By focusing on nervous system function rather than just the curve itself, we help your body communicate and adapt more effectively.

Monitoring Scoliosis Progression

Particularly for children and adolescents, monitoring scoliosis progression is crucial. Several factors influence whether a curve will progress:

  • Age and growth potential: Curves are more likely to progress during growth spurts
  • Curve magnitude: Larger curves tend to progress more than smaller ones
  • Skeletal maturity: Curves typically stabilize after skeletal maturity is reached
  • Curve location: Thoracic curves may progress differently than lumbar curves

We work alongside your medical team to ensure appropriate monitoring and will always refer for orthopedic evaluation when indicated. Our role is to support your care, not replace medical management.

Working with Your Orthopedic Team

Collaborative Care Philosophy

We believe scoliosis care is most effective as a team effort. We're happy to work alongside:

  • Orthopedic surgeons: For monitoring curve progression and determining if bracing or surgery is needed
  • Physical therapists: Especially those trained in scoliosis-specific exercises (PSSE)
  • Orthotists: If your child wears a brace, we can help support comfort and compliance
  • Primary care physicians: For overall health coordination
  • Pediatricians: For children with scoliosis

We encourage you to continue any recommended medical monitoring and treatment. Our care is designed to complement, not replace, medical management of scoliosis.

When to Seek Additional Evaluation

While providing supportive care, we're also attentive to signs that warrant immediate medical attention:

  • Rapid curve progression (more than 5 degrees between measurements)
  • Neurological symptoms such as numbness, weakness, or bowel/bladder changes
  • Severe or worsening pain not responding to conservative care
  • Significant breathing difficulties
  • Curves approaching surgical thresholds (typically 45-50 degrees)

If any of these occur, we will promptly refer you to an appropriate specialist.
