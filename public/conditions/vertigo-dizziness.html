Understanding Vertigo and Dizziness

While often used interchangeably, vertigo and dizziness describe different experiences:

What is the Difference?

  • Vertigo: A false sensation of spinning or movement, either of yourself or your surroundings. You may feel like the room is rotating around you.
  • Dizziness: A broader term that includes feeling lightheaded, unsteady, woozy, or off-balance without the spinning sensation.
  • Disequilibrium: A sense of imbalance or unsteadiness, particularly when standing or walking.

All of these can stem from problems in your vestibular system (inner ear), your brain, or importantly, your upper cervical spine.

Types of Vertigo and Dizziness

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)

The most common type of vertigo, triggered by specific head movements. Caused by tiny calcium crystals becoming dislodged in the inner ear. Episodes are typically brief but intense, often occurring when rolling over in bed or looking up.

Cervicogenic Dizziness

Dizziness that originates from problems in the neck. When upper cervical vertebrae are misaligned, they can disrupt the proprioceptive signals that help your brain understand head position, creating a mismatch with vestibular input.

Vestibular Disorders

Conditions affecting the inner ear balance organs, including vestibular neuritis, labyrinthitis, and Meniere's disease. These can cause prolonged vertigo, hearing changes, and tinnitus (ringing in the ears).

The Upper Cervical Connection

Why Your Neck Matters for Balance

Your upper cervical spine (C1 and C2 vertebrae) plays a critical role in balance and spatial orientation:

  • Proprioceptive Input: The upper neck sends continuous signals to your brain about head position, contributing to your sense of balance
  • Blood Flow: The vertebral arteries pass through the upper cervical vertebrae, supplying blood to the brain stem and cerebellum (balance centers)
  • Brain Stem Proximity: The brain stem, located just above C1, processes balance information from multiple sources
  • Neurological Integration: Signals from your eyes, inner ear, and neck must be integrated properly for normal balance

When upper cervical alignment is compromised, it can create conflicting signals that your brain interprets as dizziness or vertigo.

How Misalignment Affects Balance

Even subtle misalignments in the upper cervical spine can:

  • Alter proprioceptive signals from the neck, confusing the brain's balance centers
  • Create tension in muscles that affect head position and movement
  • Potentially affect blood flow to balance-related brain structures
  • Cause compensatory patterns throughout the spine that affect overall stability
  • Contribute to chronic muscle tension and associated symptoms

When to Seek Immediate Medical Attention

Seek Emergency Care If You Experience:

  • Sudden, severe vertigo with no clear cause
  • Vertigo accompanied by sudden severe headache
  • Difficulty speaking or slurred speech
  • Vision changes or double vision
  • Weakness or numbness on one side of the body
  • Difficulty walking or loss of coordination
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath

These symptoms could indicate a stroke or other serious condition requiring immediate medical evaluation.

For chronic or recurring vertigo and dizziness that has been evaluated by a medical professional and does not have a serious underlying cause, chiropractic care may offer significant relief.
