The Nervous System Connection

How the Brain and Bladder Communicate

Bladder control is a complex neurological process that requires proper communication between the brain, spinal cord, and bladder. During sleep, your child's brain must:

  • Receive signals from the bladder when it's full
  • Process those signals and either wake the child or signal the bladder to hold
  • Coordinate muscles that control urination
  • Maintain proper tone in the pelvic floor and sphincter muscles

When there is interference in this communication pathway, the result can be bedwetting, even in children who have been previously dry at night.

The Sacral Nerve Connection

Why the Lower Spine Matters

The nerves that control bladder function originate from the sacral region of the spine (S2-S4). These nerves are responsible for:

  • Bladder sensation: Telling the brain when the bladder is full
  • Bladder muscle control: Signaling the detrusor muscle to contract or relax
  • Sphincter control: Maintaining continence until appropriate
  • Pelvic floor coordination: Supporting proper voiding mechanics

When the sacral region of the spine experiences subluxation (misalignment causing nerve interference), it can disrupt these critical signals. This is particularly common in children who have experienced:

  • Difficult or traumatic birth
  • Falls during early childhood
  • Sports injuries
  • Prolonged sitting or poor posture

Complementary Care

Chiropractic care works well as part of a comprehensive approach. We encourage families to also consider:

  • Fluid management: Limiting fluids close to bedtime
  • Bathroom routines: Consistent voiding before sleep
  • Positive reinforcement: Celebrating dry nights without punishment for wet ones
  • Dietary considerations: Avoiding bladder irritants like caffeine and artificial sweeteners
  • Stress reduction: Addressing any underlying anxiety or stress
  • Medical evaluation: Ruling out infections or other medical causes if needed
