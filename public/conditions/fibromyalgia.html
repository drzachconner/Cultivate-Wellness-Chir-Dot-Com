Understanding Fibromyalgia

Fibromyalgia is a complex chronic pain condition characterized by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, sleep disturbances, and cognitive difficulties. While the exact cause remains under investigation, research increasingly points to changes in how the nervous system processes pain signals.

Common symptoms include:

  • Widespread pain lasting more than three months
  • Chronic fatigue and unrefreshing sleep
  • Cognitive difficulties ("fibro fog")
  • Heightened sensitivity to touch, light, sound, and temperature
  • Headaches and migraines
  • Digestive issues
  • Anxiety and depression
  • Morning stiffness

Central Sensitization and the Nervous System

The Nervous System Connection

Current research suggests that fibromyalgia involves central sensitization—a state where the central nervous system amplifies pain signals and becomes hypersensitive to stimuli that wouldn't normally cause pain.

  • Amplified pain processing: The brain and spinal cord interpret signals as more painful than they should be
  • Lowered pain threshold: Things that shouldn't hurt become painful
  • Prolonged pain response: Pain continues long after the stimulus is removed
  • Autonomic dysfunction: The nervous system struggles to regulate basic functions like sleep, digestion, and heart rate

This is why a neurologically-focused approach makes sense for fibromyalgia—we're addressing the system that's at the heart of the condition.
