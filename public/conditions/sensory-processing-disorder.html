What is Sensory Processing Disorder?

Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) occurs when the brain has difficulty receiving and responding to information that comes through the senses. While we all have sensory preferences, children with SPD experience these sensory signals in a way that significantly impacts their daily functioning, behavior, and emotional regulation.

The Nervous System Connection

SPD is fundamentally a neurological condition. The sensory signals that travel from the body to the brain must pass through the spine and brainstem. When there is interference in this pathway‚Äîsuch as from spinal subluxations‚Äîit can affect how the brain:

  • Receives sensory input: Signals may be amplified, diminished, or distorted
  • Integrates information: Difficulty combining input from multiple senses
  • Modulates responses: Over- or under-reacting to sensory stimuli
  • Regulates the autonomic nervous system: Getting "stuck" in fight-or-flight mode

By addressing spinal function and reducing nervous system interference, we support the brain's ability to process sensory information more effectively.

Types of Sensory Processing Challenges

Over-Responsive (Hypersensitive)

Children who are over-responsive perceive sensory input more intensely than others. They may:

  • Cover ears at sounds others barely notice
  • Refuse certain food textures or be extremely picky eaters
  • Become distressed by tags, seams, or certain fabrics
  • Avoid messy play or getting hands dirty
  • React strongly to bright lights or visual clutter
  • Dislike being touched, hugged, or crowded

Under-Responsive (Hyposensitive)

Children who are under-responsive may not register sensory input adequately. They may:

  • Not notice when their name is called
  • Have high pain tolerance or not notice injuries
  • Seem unaware of body in space (bumping into things)
  • Appear sluggish, tired, or disengaged
  • Need extra-strong flavors or heavy pressure to feel satisfied
  • Not notice temperature changes or wetness

Sensory Seeking

Some children actively crave intense sensory experiences. They may:

  • Constantly touch everything and everyone
  • Seek out spinning, jumping, crashing activities
  • Chew on non-food items (clothing, pencils, toys)
  • Make loud noises or prefer very loud music
  • Crave spicy, crunchy, or intense foods
  • Have difficulty sitting still

Mixed Presentation

Many children show a combination of patterns‚Äîperhaps over-responsive to sounds but under-responsive to touch, or seeking movement while avoiding certain textures. This complexity requires individualized understanding and care.

Our Sensory-Sensitive Care Approach

1. Understanding Your Child First

Before any hands-on care, we take time to understand your child's specific sensory profile:

  • Detailed discussion of triggers, preferences, and what helps them feel safe
  • Review of developmental history and birth experiences
  • Observation of how your child responds to our environment
  • Letting your child explore the space at their own pace

2. COREscore Assessment

Our non-invasive technology helps us understand your child's nervous system:

  • Heart Rate Variability (HRV) to assess autonomic balance
  • Surface EMG to evaluate muscle tension patterns
  • Thermal scanning to identify areas of nervous system stress
  • All scans are gentle and do not require uncomfortable positioning

3. Talsky Tonal Chiropractic (TTC)

Our adjusting technique is ideal for sensory-sensitive children:

  • No sudden movements: Gentle, sustained contacts
  • No loud sounds: No cracking or popping
  • Predictable: We tell your child exactly what we'll do
  • Adaptable: Can adjust with child standing, sitting, or in parent's lap
  • Respects boundaries: We always ask permission and follow your child's lead

4. Sensory-Friendly Environment

Our office is designed with sensory-sensitive children in mind:

  • Calm, low-stimulation waiting area
  • Soft lighting options
  • Minimal background noise
  • Flexible scheduling to avoid busy times if needed
  • Take-home preparation guides so children know what to expect

What Parents Notice After Care

While every child is unique and results vary, parents frequently observe improvements in:

  • Reduced sensory reactivity: Less covering ears, fewer meltdowns in stores
  • Expanded food acceptance: Willingness to try new textures
  • Easier daily routines: Less battle over clothing, grooming, transitions
  • Better sleep: Falling asleep easier, sleeping more soundly
  • Improved emotional regulation: Recovering from upsets more quickly
  • Greater body awareness: Less clumsiness, better coordination
  • More flexibility: Handling changes and new situations better
  • Calmer overall demeanor: Less "on edge" throughout the day

SPD Often Occurs Alongside Other Conditions

Sensory Processing Disorder frequently co-occurs with other neurological and developmental conditions:

  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD): Sensory differences are a core feature
  • ADHD: Sensory seeking and regulation challenges overlap
  • Anxiety disorders: Sensory overwhelm can trigger or worsen anxiety
  • Developmental coordination disorder: Body awareness and motor planning challenges
  • Learning differences: Sensory integration affects academic performance

Our neurologically-focused approach can support children regardless of their specific diagnoses by improving overall nervous system function.

Complementary Care

Chiropractic care works wonderfully alongside other therapies your child may be receiving:

  • Occupational therapy: Especially sensory integration therapy
  • Speech therapy: For oral motor and communication challenges
  • Physical therapy: For motor planning and coordination
  • Behavioral therapy: For coping strategies and regulation skills
  • Play therapy: For emotional processing
  • Dietary interventions: Addressing gut-brain connections

We're happy to coordinate with your child's therapy team to provide comprehensive support.

Why Address the Spine?

The spine houses and protects the spinal cord‚Äîthe main information highway between brain and body. Every sensory signal from the skin, muscles, and organs must travel through the spine to reach the brain. Spinal subluxations (misalignments) can create interference in these pathways, affecting how sensory information is transmitted and processed. By restoring proper spinal function, we support clearer brain-body communication.
