What is POTS/Dysautonomia?

Dysautonomia is an umbrella term for conditions involving dysfunction of the autonomic nervous system (ANS)‚Äîthe part of your nervous system that controls automatic body functions you don't consciously think about. POTS is one of the most common forms of dysautonomia.

Understanding Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction

Your autonomic nervous system controls vital functions including:

  • Heart rate and blood pressure regulation: Adjusting cardiovascular function based on position and activity
  • Blood vessel constriction: Directing blood flow where it's needed
  • Digestion: Managing the entire digestive process
  • Temperature regulation: Sweating and blood flow to maintain body temperature
  • Pupil response: Adjusting to light conditions
  • Bladder function: Controlling urination

When this system malfunctions, it can cause a wide range of symptoms affecting multiple body systems simultaneously.

POTS Specifically

In POTS, the autonomic nervous system fails to properly regulate blood flow when you change positions. When you stand up:

  • Blood pools in the lower body instead of returning to the heart efficiently
  • The heart beats much faster to compensate (often increasing 30+ beats per minute)
  • Blood pressure may drop or fluctuate
  • The brain may not receive adequate blood flow, causing dizziness and cognitive symptoms

The Autonomic Nervous System Explained

Your autonomic nervous system has two main branches that must work in balance:

Sympathetic Nervous System ("Fight or Flight")

Activates during stress or activity:

  • Increases heart rate and blood pressure
  • Diverts blood to muscles
  • Heightens alertness
  • Slows digestion

Parasympathetic Nervous System ("Rest and Digest")

Promotes recovery and calm:

  • Slows heart rate
  • Supports digestion
  • Promotes healing and recovery
  • Conserves energy

In dysautonomia, these two systems don't communicate properly or respond appropriately to the body's needs. Many people with POTS are stuck in sympathetic overdrive‚Äîtheir bodies act as if they're constantly under stress, even at rest.

The Chiropractic Connection

Upper Cervical Spine & the Brain Stem

The upper cervical spine (C1-C2) sits directly beneath the brain stem‚Äîthe control center for autonomic function. Misalignments in this area can:

  • Create pressure or irritation affecting brain stem function
  • Alter blood flow to and from the brain
  • Disrupt cerebrospinal fluid dynamics
  • Affect the vagus nerve and autonomic signaling

The Vagus Nerve Connection

The vagus nerve is the primary nerve of your parasympathetic system, running from the brain stem through the neck and into the body. It influences:

  • Heart rate regulation
  • Blood pressure control
  • Digestive function
  • Inflammation response
  • Overall autonomic tone

The vagus nerve passes through the upper cervical region, making the alignment and function of this area particularly important for people with autonomic dysfunction.
