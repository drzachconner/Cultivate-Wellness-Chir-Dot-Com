What Is Torticollis?

Understanding the Condition

Torticollis is a condition where the muscles on one side of the neck become tight or shortened, causing the baby's head to tilt to one side while rotating toward the opposite shoulder. This creates a characteristic head position that limits normal range of motion.

There are two main types:

  • Congenital Muscular Torticollis (CMT): Present at birth or develops within the first few weeks of life
  • Acquired Torticollis: Develops later due to positioning habits or other factors

Approximately 1 in 250 infants are born with some degree of torticollis, making it one of the most common musculoskeletal conditions in newborns.

What Causes Torticollis in Infants?

Birth Trauma

The birth process, while natural, can place significant stress on a baby's delicate neck and spine. Contributing factors include:

  • Prolonged or difficult labor
  • Use of vacuum extraction or forceps
  • Breech presentation or abnormal positioning
  • Emergency C-section after failed vaginal delivery
  • Rapid delivery causing sudden positional changes
  • Large baby size relative to birth canal

Even routine births can result in subtle misalignments that affect muscle tension and nerve function in the neck.

In-Utero Positioning

How your baby was positioned during pregnancy can contribute to torticollis:

  • Crowded uterine environment (multiples, low amniotic fluid)
  • Consistent positioning on one side
  • Breech position for extended periods
  • Uterine abnormalities limiting movement

Positional Factors After Birth

Post-birth habits can develop or worsen torticollis:

  • Extended time in car seats, swings, or bouncers
  • Always being held or fed on the same side
  • Preference for looking at one direction (toward light, sounds, or parents)
  • Limited tummy time

Why Early Intervention Matters

The Importance of Acting Early

Addressing torticollis promptly is crucial because:

  • Infant tissues are more adaptable: Younger babies respond faster to care
  • Prevents secondary issues: Untreated torticollis can lead to plagiocephaly (flat head syndrome)
  • Supports development: Normal neck movement is essential for motor milestone achievement
  • Reduces treatment duration: Earlier intervention typically means shorter treatment time
  • Improves feeding: Balanced neck movement supports better breastfeeding outcomes

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends early evaluation and intervention for best outcomes.
