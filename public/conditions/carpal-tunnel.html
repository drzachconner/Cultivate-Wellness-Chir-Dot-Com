What is Carpal Tunnel Syndrome?

Understanding the Anatomy

Carpal tunnel syndrome occurs when the median nerve becomes compressed as it passes through the carpal tunnel‚Äîa narrow passageway on the palm side of your wrist. This nerve:

  • Originates in the cervical spine: Nerve roots from C5-T1 form the brachial plexus, which gives rise to the median nerve
  • Travels through the arm: Running from the neck, through the shoulder, down the arm to the hand
  • Controls sensation: Provides feeling to the thumb, index, middle, and half of the ring finger
  • Enables movement: Controls muscles at the base of the thumb for grip and pinch

This is why addressing only the wrist may not resolve symptoms‚Äîthe nerve can be irritated or compressed anywhere along its path.

Common Causes

Carpal tunnel syndrome can develop from multiple factors:

  • Repetitive motions: Typing, assembly work, using vibrating tools
  • Prolonged wrist positions: Flexed or extended wrist during sleep or work
  • Cervical spine dysfunction: Misalignments affecting nerve roots
  • Double crush syndrome: Nerve irritation at multiple points along its path
  • Inflammatory conditions: Rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes
  • Hormonal changes: Pregnancy, menopause, thyroid disorders
  • Fluid retention: Causing swelling in the carpal tunnel
  • Trauma or injury: Wrist fractures, dislocations

The Cervical Spine Connection

Double Crush Syndrome

Research has shown that when a nerve is compressed or irritated at one point, it becomes more vulnerable to compression at other points along its path. This is called "double crush syndrome."

In many carpal tunnel cases:

  • The nerve roots are already stressed at the cervical spine
  • This makes the median nerve more susceptible to compression at the wrist
  • Treating only the wrist ignores half the problem
  • Addressing cervical spine issues can significantly reduce wrist symptoms

How Neck Problems Contribute

The cervical spine plays a critical role in hand and wrist function:

  • Nerve root irritation: Cervical subluxations can compress or irritate nerve roots before they even reach the arm
  • Postural stress: Forward head posture and rounded shoulders increase tension throughout the nerve pathway
  • Reduced nerve mobility: Spinal restrictions can limit normal nerve gliding, creating additional stress
  • Referred symptoms: Cervical radiculopathy can mimic or compound carpal tunnel symptoms

Prevention Strategies

Protecting Your Hands and Wrists

Whether you're seeking to prevent carpal tunnel or avoid recurrence, consider these strategies:

  • Ergonomic workspace: Keep wrists in neutral position while typing; use proper keyboard and mouse placement
  • Take breaks: Rest your hands every 15-20 minutes during repetitive tasks
  • Stretch regularly: Wrist flexor and extensor stretches throughout the day
  • Mind your posture: Avoid forward head posture that stresses the cervical spine
  • Sleep position: Avoid sleeping with wrists bent; consider a supportive pillow arrangement
  • Stay warm: Cold hands are more prone to stiffness and symptoms
  • Reduce force: Use less grip pressure when possible; choose ergonomic tools
  • Regular chiropractic care: Maintain cervical spine alignment and nerve function
