What Is Tech Neck?

Tech neck (also called "text neck" or "forward head posture") is a modern postural syndrome caused by the repetitive stress of looking down at screens, phones, tablets, and computers. Over time, this position causes:

  • Forward migration of the head relative to the shoulders
  • Rounding of the upper back (kyphosis)
  • Tight, shortened muscles in the front of the neck
  • Weak, overstretched muscles in the back of the neck and upper back
  • Loss of the natural cervical curve
  • Increased stress on spinal discs, joints, and nerves

Modern Posture Problems

Tech neck is just one aspect of the postural crisis we face today. Other common issues include:

Sedentary Lifestyle Effects

  • Prolonged sitting: Weakens core and gluteal muscles, tightens hip flexors
  • Desk work posture: Shoulders rolled forward, chin jutting out
  • Couch slouching: Compression of the lumbar spine
  • Car commuting: Hours in unsupportive positions

Common Postural Patterns We See

  • Forward head carriage: Head positioned in front of shoulders
  • Rounded shoulders: Protracted scapulae and tight pectorals
  • Hyperkyphosis: Excessive upper back curvature ("hunchback")
  • Anterior pelvic tilt: Excessive lower back curve from tight hip flexors
  • Sway back posture: Hips pushed forward with flattened lower back

Effects on Spine & Nervous System

  • Muscle imbalance: Some muscles become tight, others weak and inhibited
  • Reduced lung capacity: Slouching limits breathing and oxygen intake
  • Impaired circulation: Poor posture restricts blood flow
  • Mood and energy effects: Research links posture to depression and fatigue

Forward Head Posture: A Closer Look

Forward head posture (FHP) deserves special attention because it's so prevalent and has such significant effects:

How FHP Develops

  1. Repeated looking down at devices
  2. Suboccipital muscles (base of skull) tighten to keep eyes level
  3. Deep neck flexors become weak and inhibited
  4. Upper cervical joints become hypermobile (unstable)
  5. Lower cervical joints become hypomobile (restricted)
  6. Pattern becomes "locked in" over time

Why It Matters

The upper cervical spine is particularly critical because:

  • It houses the brain stem‚Äîthe "master control center" for vital functions
  • Blood flow to the brain passes through vertebral arteries here
  • The vagus nerve, affecting digestion and relaxation, originates nearby
  • Proprioceptive input (body position sense) is dense in this region
  • Misalignment here affects the entire spine's compensation patterns

Children and Screens

A Growing Concern for Our Kids

Children are particularly vulnerable to tech neck and postural problems:

  • Developing spines: Still forming and highly adaptable‚Äîfor better or worse
  • Earlier exposure: Children now use devices from toddler age
  • More screen time: Average child: 5-7 hours daily
  • Heavier heads: Children's heads are proportionally larger relative to their bodies
  • Weaker musculature: Still developing neck and postural muscles
  • Poor ergonomics: Adult-sized furniture and devices

Early intervention is crucial. Postural patterns established in childhood often persist into adulthood and become progressively harder to correct.

Signs Your Child May Have Posture Problems

  • Head noticeably forward when standing naturally
  • Rounded shoulders or "hunched" appearance
  • Complaints of neck or back pain
  • Headaches, especially after screen use
  • Difficulty sitting up straight
  • One shoulder higher than the other
  • Fatigue and difficulty concentrating

Exercises & Tips for Better Posture

Ergonomic Quick Wins

  • Screen at eye level: Top of monitor at or slightly below eye height
  • Phone posture: Bring device to face, don't bring face to device
  • Take breaks: Every 20-30 minutes, look up and move
  • Standing desk option: Alternate between sitting and standing
  • Supportive chair: Lumbar support, feet flat on floor
  • Sleep position: Proper pillow height to maintain neutral neck

Chin Tucks

Gently draw your chin straight back (like making a "double chin") while keeping eyes level. Hold 5 seconds, repeat 10 times. This strengthens deep neck flexors and counteracts forward head posture.

Doorway Stretch

Stand in a doorway with arms at 90 degrees on the frame. Step forward gently until you feel a stretch across your chest and front of shoulders. Hold 30 seconds. Helps open tight pectorals from rounding forward.

Scapular Squeezes

Squeeze shoulder blades together and slightly down, as if trying to put them in your back pockets. Hold 5 seconds, repeat 10 times. Strengthens rhomboids and lower traps.

Wall Angels

Stand with back against wall, arms in "goalpost" position. Slowly slide arms up and down while keeping contact with wall. 10 repetitions. Great for thoracic mobility and posture awareness.

Thoracic Extension

Using a foam roller placed horizontally across mid-back, gently extend backward over it. Move roller to different levels of thoracic spine. Helps restore upper back curve.

Neck Stretches

Gently tilt ear toward shoulder, hold 30 seconds each side. For deeper stretch, place hand on opposite side of head with light pressure. Releases tight upper trapezius and scalenes.
